\section*{Why this paper matters for the formalization}
This survey supplies modern notation and clean statements for Hilbert's metric on cones, order-preserving homogeneous maps, Birkhoff's contraction ratio, and the finite positive-matrix formula for projective diameter.  It is the best source for translating Birkhoff's argument into Lean-friendly finite-dimensional statements.

\section*{Core definitions}
Let \(C\) be a cone in a real vector space.  For \(x\in V\), \(y\in C\), define
\[
  M(x/y)=\inf\{\beta : x\le_C \beta y\},\qquad
  m(x/y)=\sup\{\alpha : \alpha y\le_C x\}.
\]
For comparable nonzero points in the same part of the cone, Birkhoff's Hilbert metric is
\[
  d(x,y)=\log\frac{M(x/y)}{m(x/y)}.
\]
For the positive cone \(\mathbb{R}_+^n\) and strictly positive vectors,
\[
  M(x/y)=\max_i \frac{x_i}{y_i},\qquad
  m(x/y)=\min_i \frac{x_i}{y_i}.
\]
Thus \(d(x,y)=0\) exactly when \(x\) and \(y\) are positive scalar multiples.

\section*{Metric and nonexpansive-map facts}
The survey proves the basic metric properties:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
\item nonnegativity, symmetry, and triangle inequality for \(d\) on each part;
\item projective invariance \(d(\lambda x,\mu y)=d(x,y)\) for \(\lambda,\mu>0\);
\item in a closed Banach cone, zero distance means equality of rays.
\end{enumerate}
If \(f:C\to K\) is order-preserving and homogeneous of degree \(r>0\), then
\[
  M(f(x)/f(y))\le M(x/y)^r,
  \qquad
  m(x/y)^r\le m(f(x)/f(y)).
\]
Therefore degree-one order-preserving maps are nonexpansive for \(d\).  Order-reversing homogeneous maps of degree \(-1\) are also nonexpansive after the corresponding inversion of inequalities.

\section*{Birkhoff contraction ratio}
For a positive linear map \(L:C\to K\), define
\[
  \kappa(L)=\inf\{c: d(Lx,Ly)\le c\,d(x,y)\},\qquad
  \Delta(L)=\sup\{d(Lx,Ly):x,y\in C\}.
\]
Birkhoff's theorem states
\[
  \kappa(L)=\tanh\bigl(\Delta(L)/4\bigr),
\]
with \(\tanh(\infty)=1\).  Hence finite projective diameter implies strict contraction.

For a strictly positive matrix \(A=(a_{ij})\), the survey records the explicit finite formula
\[
  \Delta(A)=\log\max_{i,j,p,q}\frac{a_{pi}a_{qj}}{a_{pj}a_{qi}} < \infty.
\]
This formula is especially useful for Lean because it avoids compactness of function spaces and reduces finite diameter to inequalities over finite index sets.

\section*{Distilled proof pattern for finite matrices}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
\item Strict positivity of all entries gives every image coordinate \((Ax)_p=\sum_i a_{pi}x_i\) positive for nonzero \(x\ge0\).
\item For two rows \(p,q\), compare \((Ax)_p/(Ax)_q\) using the coordinatewise ratios \(a_{pi}/a_{qi}\).  The ratio of two image vectors is trapped between finite extrema over entries of \(A\).
\item The logarithmic spread of these extrema gives finite projective diameter \(\Delta(A)\).
\item Birkhoff's theorem turns finite diameter into a strict contraction of Hilbert distance.
\end{enumerate}

\section*{Lean comparison target}
For \texttt{ProjectiveLag.lean}, the most directly formalizable lemma is not the full theorem \(\kappa=\tanh(\Delta/4)\), but the finite diameter estimate:
\[

  d_H(Kx,Ky)\le
  \log\max_{i,i',j,j'}\frac{G_{ij}G_{i'j'}}{G_{ij'}G_{i'j}}.

\section*{Projective-lag relevance}
The two remaining Lean targets are cross-product limits.  In a finite positive cone,
\[
  d_H(u_n,u_{n+1})\to 0
\]
implies for all \(i,j\),
\[
  \frac{u_{n+1,i}}{u_{n,i}}-\frac{u_{n+1,j}}{u_{n,j}}\to 0,
\]
and with positive box bounds this is equivalent to
\[
  u_{n+1,i}u_{n,j}-u_{n+1,j}u_{n,i}\to 0.
\]
