\section*{Reference being distilled}
This note distills the final part of Joel Franklin and Jens Lorenz,
\emph{On the Scaling of Multidimensional Matrices}, Linear Algebra and its Applications
114/115:717--735, 1989 \cite{FranklinLorenz1989}.  The exact source section is
Section 3, ``Hilbert's Projective Metric and Sinkhorn's Iteration'', pp. 725--732
of the article.  The core convergence result is Theorem 4 on pp. 729--731, preceded
by the Hilbert metric setup and Lemmas 1--2 on pp. 726--729.

\section*{Purpose of this distillation}
The surrounding formalization needs a finite positive-kernel asymptotic-regularity
argument for Sinkhorn-type iterates.  Franklin--Lorenz do not merely cite Sinkhorn's
iteration: in Section 3 they isolate the Hilbert-projective contraction mechanism,
prove geometric contraction of row/column marginal errors, and convert those bounds
into convergence in a metric on the diagonal-equivalence class of the matrix.  This is
one of the closest literature sources to the remaining Lean projective-lag frontier.

\section*{Notation normalized for formalization}
Let $A=(a_{ij})$ be a strictly positive $m\times n$ matrix.  Let
$p\in\mathbb{R}^m_{>0}$ and $q\in\mathbb{R}^n_{>0}$ have the same total mass.
Write $e$ for the all-ones vector of the relevant dimension.

The Sinkhorn row/column-normalization cycle is:
\[
  r^{(k)} := A_k e, \qquad
  A'_k := S_k A_k, \qquad
  S_k := \operatorname{diag}\left(\frac{p_i}{r^{(k)}_i}\right),
\]
\[
  c^{(k)} := (A'_k)^T e, \qquad
  A_{k+1} := A'_k T_k, \qquad
  T_k := \operatorname{diag}\left(\frac{q_j}{c^{(k)}_j}\right).
\]
Thus $A'_k$ has row sums $p$ and $A_{k+1}$ has column sums $q$.
Franklin--Lorenz assume in Theorem 4 that the starting matrix is already column
normalized, $A^T e=q$, so that $A_0^T e=q$.

For $x,x'\in\mathbb{R}^m_{>0}$, Hilbert's projective metric is
\[
  d_H(x,x') = \log \max_{i,k}\frac{x_i x'_k}{x'_i x_k}
            = \log\frac{\max_i(x_i/x'_i)}{\min_i(x_i/x'_i)}.
\]
It is a metric on positive rays: $d_H(x,x')=0$ iff $x=\alpha x'$ for some
$\alpha>0$.

For a positive matrix $A$, Franklin--Lorenz use the multiplicative projective
diameter
\[
  \vartheta(A)
  := \max_{i,j,k,\ell}\frac{a_{ik}a_{j\ell}}{a_{jk}a_{i\ell}}.
\]
Equivalently, the additive Hilbert diameter is $\log\vartheta(A)$.  Their Lemma 1
states the contraction ratio
\[
  \kappa(A)
  = \sup_{y\not\sim y'} \frac{d_H(Ay,Ay')}{d_H(y,y')}
  = \frac{\sqrt{\vartheta(A)}-1}{\sqrt{\vartheta(A)}+1}
  = \tanh\!\left(\frac{\log\vartheta(A)}{4}\right).
\]
The formula is invariant under positive diagonal equivalence of $A$.

\section*{Distilled proof of the Section 3 convergence argument}

\begin{distillation}
\item \textbf{Positive matrices are strict Hilbert contractions.}
Because $A$ is strictly positive and finite dimensional, $\vartheta(A)<\infty$ and
therefore $0\le \kappa(A)<1$.  This is the Birkhoff input.  Positive diagonal row or
column scalings do not change $\vartheta(A)$, hence every matrix $A_k$ and $A'_k$ in
the Sinkhorn iteration has the same contraction ratio as the original $A$.

\item \textbf{One full Sinkhorn cycle contracts the row and column marginal errors.}
Suppose $A_0$ is column normalized, so $A_0^T e=q$.  Let $\gamma:=\kappa(A)^2$.
Franklin--Lorenz Lemma 2 proves
\[
  d_H(r^{(1)},p) \le \gamma\,d_H(r^{(0)},p),
  \qquad
  d_H(c^{(1)},q) \le \gamma\,d_H(c^{(0)},q).
\]
The proof is two applications of Birkhoff contraction, one through $A'_0$ and one
through $(A'_0)^T$.  The elementary identity
\[
  d_H(x/x',e)=d_H(x,x')
\]
turns the diagonal correction vectors $q/c^{(1)}$ and $p/r^{(0)}$ into marginal-error
distances.  Iterating Lemma 2 gives
\[
  d_H(r^{(k)},p) \le \gamma^k d_H(r^{(0)},p),
  \qquad
  d_H(c^{(k)},q) \le \gamma^k d_H(c^{(0)},q).
\]

\item \textbf{The matrix-distance increment is exactly the sum of the two marginal-error
corrections.}
Let $E_A$ be the positive diagonal-equivalence class of $A$.  If
$B=XB'Y$ with $X=\operatorname{diag}(x_i)$ and $Y=\operatorname{diag}(y_j)$,
Franklin--Lorenz define
\[
  \Delta(B,B') := d_H(x,e)+d_H(y,e).
\]
For consecutive Sinkhorn matrices,
\[
  A_{k+1}=S_k A_k T_k,
\]
so
\[
  \Delta(A_k,A_{k+1})
  = d_H(p/r^{(k)},e)+d_H(q/c^{(k)},e)
  = d_H(r^{(k)},p)+d_H(c^{(k)},q).
\]
The last equality is exactly the quotient-to-Hilbert-metric identity above.

\item \textbf{The geometric tail gives convergence in $\Delta$.}
Theorem 4 sums the previous increment estimate.  If $\widehat B$ is the unique
matrix diagonally equivalent to $A$ with row sums $p$ and column sums $q$, then
\[
  \Delta(A_k,\widehat B)
  \le \frac{1}{1-\gamma}
      \left(d_H(r^{(k)},p)+d_H(c^{(k)},q)\right)
  \le \frac{\gamma^k}{1-\gamma}
      \left(d_H(r^{(0)},p)+d_H(c^{(0)},q)\right).
\]
Franklin--Lorenz also state the analogous estimate for $A'_k$:
\[
  \Delta(A'_k,\widehat B)
  \le \frac{1}{1-\gamma}
      \left(d_H(r^{(k+1)},p)+d_H(c^{(k)},q)\right).
\]
Thus $A_k$ and $A'_k$ converge geometrically to the same scaled matrix.

\item \textbf{Completeness can replace a prior convergence theorem.}
The paper notes that its displayed proof uses Sinkhorn's earlier convergence theorem
for the existence of $\widehat B$, but also observes that if one proves completeness of
$(E_A,\Delta)$, then the same geometric-tail argument independently gives the limit
and convergence in $(E_A,\Delta)$.

\item \textbf{Entrywise error bounds are a consequence, not the main projective step.}
Lemma 3 states that if $A\sim B$, $A^Te=B^Te$, and $\Delta(A,B)\le\epsilon$, then
\[
  e^{-\epsilon}\le \frac{b_{ij}}{a_{ij}}\le e^{\epsilon}.
\]
This converts the projective/diagonal-equivalence estimate into entrywise multiplicative
control.  It is useful for numerical error bounds, but the formal compactness frontier
mainly needs the preceding projective asymptotic regularity.
\end{distillation}

\section*{Formalization consequences}
\paragraph{Useful theorem to port.}
The exact statement most useful for Lean is not necessarily entrywise convergence.
It is the geometric marginal-error contraction:
\[
  d_H(r^{(k)},p) \to 0,\qquad d_H(c^{(k)},q)\to 0,
\]
and the increment identity
\[
  \Delta(A_k,A_{k+1}) = d_H(r^{(k)},p)+d_H(c^{(k)},q) \to 0.
\]
Together these imply projective asymptotic regularity of the diagonal correction
vectors:
\[
  d_H(p/r^{(k)},e)\to 0,\qquad d_H(q/c^{(k)},e)\to 0.
\]
In coordinates, for a positive vector stream $u_k$, projective convergence to a ray is
equivalent to cross-product collapse:
\[
  u_{k,i}u_{k-1,j}-u_{k,j}u_{k-1,i}\to 0
\]
after the stream is identified with the appropriate row or column diagonal scaling
sequence.  This is the bridge to the current projective-lag predicates.

\paragraph{Hypotheses that must be present.}
Franklin--Lorenz Section 3 requires a strictly positive finite matrix $A$ and positive
prescribed marginals $p,q$.  The Birkhoff constant is strictly less than $1$ precisely
because the multiplicative diameter $\vartheta(A)$ is finite.  In Lean, this suggests that
any theorem trying to import this argument should have access to the positive-kernel
hypothesis
\[
  \forall i j,\;0<G_{ij},
\]
not merely phase box bounds.  The phase bounds keep vectors in the positive cone, but
the strict contraction input is a property of the kernel matrix.

\paragraph{What Franklin--Lorenz proves and what remains to adapt.}
Franklin--Lorenz prove convergence of matrices in a diagonal-equivalence class.  The
current finite Schr\"odinger/Sinkhorn formalization works with phase variables rather
than explicitly with matrices $A_k=S_kAT_k$.  To use this source cleanly, the Lean proof
should either:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
\item build the implicit normalized matrices from the phase iterates and show that their
row/column normalization cycle matches Franklin--Lorenz's $A_k,A'_k$; or
\item port only the vector-level contraction lemma behind their Lemma 2, stated directly
for the phase update maps.
\end{enumerate}
The second route is probably closer to the existing Lean files, while the first route is
closer to the published theorem.

\section*{Caution about the exact source theorem}
Theorem 4 is a convergence theorem for full alternating normalized matrices, not a
standalone theorem about arbitrary bounded positive sequences.  It cannot justify
projective-lag collapse unless the formal streams are first shown to be generated by the
same row/column normalization cycle.  This is precisely why the Sinkhorn iterate-system
hypotheses and positive-kernel hypothesis are essential in the Lean theorem shape.
