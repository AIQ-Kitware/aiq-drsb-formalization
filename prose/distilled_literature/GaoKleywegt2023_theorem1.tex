\section{Primal and dual problems}
Let $(\Xi,d)$ be Polish, $p\in[1,\infty)$, $\nu\in\Pcal_p(\Xi)$, $\theta>0$, and let
$\Psi:\Xi\to\R$ be $\nu$-integrable.  Define
\[
 v_P:=\sup_{\mu\in\Pcal(\Xi):W_p(\mu,\nu)\le\theta}\int\Psi\,d\mu
\]
and
\[
 v_D:=\inf_{\lambda\ge0}
 \left\{\lambda\theta^p-
   \int \Phi(\lambda,\zeta)\,\nu(d\zeta)\right\},
 \qquad
 \Phi(\lambda,\zeta):=
 \inf_{\xi\in\Xi}\{\lambda d^p(\xi,\zeta)-\Psi(\xi)\}.
\]
The sign convention makes $-\Phi$ the penalized robust envelope.

\section{Growth rate and near-minimizers}
Define
\[
 \kappa:=\inf\left\{\lambda\ge0:
         \int\Phi(\lambda,\zeta)\,\nu(d\zeta)>-\infty\right\}.
\]
When $\Xi$ is unbounded, $\kappa$ is the asymptotic $d^p$-growth rate of $\Psi$:
\[
 \kappa=
 \limsup_{d^p(\xi,\zeta)\to\infty}
 \frac{\max\{0,\Psi(\xi)-\Psi(\zeta)\}}{d^p(\xi,\zeta)},
\]
independently of the base point $\zeta$.  Equivalently, $\kappa<\infty$ exactly when there exist
$L,M>0$ and $\zeta_0$ such that
\[
 \Psi(\xi)-\Psi(\zeta_0)\le Ld^p(\xi,\zeta_0)+M.
\]
For $\Phi(\lambda,\zeta)>-\infty$, define the limiting largest and smallest transport costs among
near-minimizers:
\begin{align*}
 D^+(\lambda,\zeta)
 &:=\lim_{\varepsilon\downarrow0}
   \sup\{d^p(\xi,\zeta):
      \lambda d^p(\xi,\zeta)-\Psi(\xi)
      \le\Phi(\lambda,\zeta)+\varepsilon\},\\
 D^-(\lambda,\zeta)
 &:=\lim_{\varepsilon\downarrow0}
   \inf\{d^p(\xi,\zeta):
      \lambda d^p(\xi,\zeta)-\Psi(\xi)
      \le\Phi(\lambda,\zeta)+\varepsilon\}.
\end{align*}
The measurable-selection lemmas provide measurable near-minimizers
$T^+_{\varepsilon,\delta}(\zeta)$ and $T^-_{\varepsilon,\delta}(\zeta)$ whose transport costs are,
respectively, within $\delta$ of $D^+$ and $D^-$.  When actual minimizers exist, the same holds with
exact argmin selectors.  The proof uses measurable-graph results for normal integrands on Polish
spaces.

\section{Weak duality}
Given a coupling $\pi\in\Pi(\mu,\nu)$ and $\lambda\ge0$,
\[
 \Psi(\xi)\le\lambda d^p(\xi,\zeta)-\Phi(\lambda,\zeta).
\]
Integrating and using $\int d^p\,d\pi\le\theta^p$ gives
\[
 \int\Psi\,d\mu
 \le\lambda\theta^p-
   \int\Phi(\lambda,\zeta)\,\nu(d\zeta).
\]
Taking the supremum over $\mu$ and then the infimum over $\lambda$ yields $v_P\le v_D$.

\section{Properties of the scalar dual objective}
Write
\[
 h(\lambda):=\lambda\theta^p-
             \int\Phi(\lambda,\zeta)\,\nu(d\zeta).
\]
For each $\zeta$, $\Phi(\cdot,\zeta)$ is concave and nondecreasing, hence $h$ is convex.  It is
finite on $(\kappa,\infty)$, lower semicontinuous on $[0,\infty)$, and coercive when the radius is
positive.  Therefore, if $\kappa<\infty$, the dual infimum is attained at some
$\lambda^*\in[\kappa,\infty)$.

The one-sided derivatives of $-\Phi$ are governed by the extreme costs of near-minimizers.  After
integration,
\begin{align*}
 h'_-(\lambda)&=\theta^p-
      \int D^+(\lambda,\zeta)\,\nu(d\zeta),\\
 h'_+(\lambda)&=\theta^p-
      \int D^-(\lambda,\zeta)\,\nu(d\zeta)
\end{align*}
whenever $\lambda>\kappa$, with the analogous inequalities sufficient at boundary points.  At an
interior minimizer,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:GK-budget-bracket}
 \int D^-(\lambda^*,\zeta)\,d\nu
 \le\theta^p\le
 \int D^+(\lambda^*,\zeta)\,d\nu.
\end{equation}

\section{Constructing a nearly optimal primal law when $\lambda^*>\kappa$}
Fix small $\varepsilon,\delta>0$.  Select measurable maps $T^+$ and $T^-$ satisfying
\begin{align*}
 \lambda^*d^p(T^\pm(\zeta),\zeta)-\Psi(T^\pm(\zeta))
 &\le\Phi(\lambda^*,\zeta)+\varepsilon,\\
 d^p(T^+(\zeta),\zeta)&\ge D^+(\lambda^*,\zeta)-\delta,\\
 d^p(T^-(\zeta),\zeta)&\le D^-(\lambda^*,\zeta)+\delta.
\end{align*}
By \eqref{eq:GK-budget-bracket}, after a vanishing adjustment of the selectors there exists
$q\in[0,1]$ such that
\[
 q\int d^p(T^+(\zeta),\zeta)\,d\nu
 +(1-q)\int d^p(T^-(\zeta),\zeta)\,d\nu
 \le\theta^p
\]
and the left side approaches $\theta^p$ as the errors vanish.  Define a coupling by
\[
 \pi_{\varepsilon,\delta}(d\xi,d\zeta)
 :=\bigl[q\delta_{T^+(\zeta)}(d\xi)
 +(1-q)\delta_{T^-(\zeta)}(d\xi)\bigr]\nu(d\zeta).
\]
Its second marginal is $\nu$, its first marginal $\mu_{\varepsilon,\delta}$ lies in the Wasserstein
ball, and the near-minimizer inequalities give
\begin{align*}
 \int\Psi\,d\mu_{\varepsilon,\delta}
 &\ge \lambda^*\int d^p\,d\pi_{\varepsilon,\delta}
       -\int\Phi(\lambda^*,\zeta)\,d\nu-\varepsilon\\
 &\longrightarrow
 \lambda^*\theta^p-
       \int\Phi(\lambda^*,\zeta)\,d\nu
 =v_D.
\end{align*}
Thus $v_P\ge v_D$.

\section{The boundary case $\lambda^*=\kappa$}
At the growth boundary, the preceding selector construction can fail because near-minimizers may
escape to infinity.  The proof perturbs the multiplier to values on both sides of the minimizer that
remain above $\kappa$, takes measurable near-minimizers for those perturbed problems, and mixes
them with the identity map $\zeta\mapsto\zeta$.  Convex weights are chosen so that:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item the total mass is one;
\item the expected $p$-transport cost is at most $\theta^p$;
\item the Lagrangian defect tends to zero as the perturbations vanish.
\end{enumerate}
If the minimizing sequence escapes to infinity, the definition of $\kappa$ guarantees that the payoff
growth along that sequence compensates exactly for the boundary transport penalty.  The resulting
three-component coupling again has objective converging to $v_D$.  Hence $v_P\ge v_D$ also at
$\lambda^*=\kappa$.

If $\kappa=\infty$, for every candidate multiplier there are measurable points where the payoff
outgrows the transport penalty.  Moving an arbitrarily small amount of mass to those points produces
feasible laws with arbitrarily large expected payoff.  Therefore $v_P=v_D=+\infty$.

\begin{theorem}[Strong duality]
If $\kappa<\infty$, then
\[
 v_P=v_D<\infty,
\]
and the scalar dual problem admits an optimizer $\lambda^*\ge\kappa$.  If $\kappa=\infty$, both
values are $+\infty$.
\end{theorem}

\section{Worst-case distributions}
Suppose the pointwise penalized problems attain their minima at $\lambda^*$.  Let
$T^+$ and $T^-$ be measurable exact argmin selectors with the largest and smallest available
transport costs, and choose $q$ so that the resulting mixture has cost $\theta^p$.  Then
\[
 \pi^*(d\xi,d\zeta)
 :=\bigl[q\delta_{T^+(\zeta)}(d\xi)
 +(1-q)\delta_{T^-(\zeta)}(d\xi)\bigr]\nu(d\zeta)
\]
is optimal, and its first marginal is a worst-case distribution.  Conversely, every optimal coupling
must be concentrated on
\[
 \argmin_{\xi\in\Xi}
 \{\lambda^*d^p(\xi,\zeta)-\Psi(\xi\}
\]
and must satisfy complementary slackness for the transport budget.

For an empirical nominal law
$\nu=N^{-1}\sum_{i=1}^N\delta_{\widehat\xi_i}$, one can choose a worst-case law supported on at
most $2N$ points:
\[
 \mu^*=\frac1N\sum_{i=1}^N
 \bigl[p_i\delta_{\xi_i^+}+(1-p_i)\delta_{\xi_i^-}\bigr],
\]
where each $\xi_i^\pm$ solves the corresponding penalized point problem.  Optimizing the weights
$p_i$ is a linear program with one transport-budget constraint.  An extreme optimal solution has at
most one fractional $p_i$, so a worst-case law can be represented by at most $N+1$ effective atoms
when coincident and zero-weight atoms are removed.

\section{Logical content of the proof}
The strong-duality proof is constructive.  Convexity of the scalar dual objective produces a bracket
for the desired transport budget; measurable near-minimizers realize the two sides of the bracket;
and a pointwise two-way mixture interpolates to the prescribed radius.  The only additional issue is
the boundary growth rate, where a third, untransported component controls escape to infinity.
